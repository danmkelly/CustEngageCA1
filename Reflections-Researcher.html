2. Thought Process: How the Audit Was Built

Step 1: Frame Selection

I began by selecting the master diagnostic framework. The Mayer-Davis-Schoorman (1995) trust construct (ability, benevolence, integrity) was chosen as the primary lens because:

  • It is the core trust framework of the H9CEAI module (Week 2, V2).
  • It disaggregates trust into three independently failing dimensions, which forces precision. Saying "passengers do not trust CloudNine" is diagnostically useless. Saying "ability is adequate, benevolence is failing, integrity is at risk on the loyalty programme" is actionable.
  • It maps cleanly onto the Lee and See (2004) calibration curve (Week 2, V3) and Burrell's (2016) opacity types, creating a stacked diagnostic vocabulary.

Step 2: Challenge-by-Challenge Mapping

For each of the three named challenges, I applied the stacked framework:

  1. Name the trust dimension (Mayer-Davis-Schoorman). Which of ability, benevolence, or integrity is failing, and what observable evidence (from the Task 1 document) supports that?
  2. Name the calibration state (Lee and See). Is the passenger over-trusting, under-trusting, or calibrated? What evidence supports this?
  3. Name the opacity type (Burrell). Is the opacity intentional (a design choice), technical (a model limitation), or scale-and-complexity (a data-volume problem)?
  4. Map to DART (Prahalad and Ramaswamy). Which of Dialogue, Access, Risk, or Transparency is failing?
  5. Check for bias mechanisms (Barocas and Selbst). Which of the five bias surfaces is exposed?
  6. Check for dark patterns (Brignull, Gray et al.). Which of the 16 deceptive design types are likely present?

Step 3: Synthesis Across Challenges

I looked for interdependence. The three challenges were presented sequentially in the Task 1 document, but the trust construct framework suggests they interact systemically. The "self-reinforcing trust deficit loop" emerged from asking: "If Challenge 2 (opaque ancillaries) is the initial breach, what does it do to Challenge 1 (loyalty engagement)? And what does reduced loyalty engagement do to Challenge 3 (AI data quality)?" This synthesis step is the primary value-add of the Research Agent over a simple restatement of the Task 1 document.

Step 4: Prioritisation Logic

The three priorities were ranked using the following criteria (derived from the Researcher archetype's evaluation-mode stance):

  • Proximity to revenue: Which intervention, if successful, most directly impacts ancillary revenue?
  • Signal speed: Which intervention produces passenger-visible change fastest?
  • Trust dimension coverage: Which intervention addresses the most dimensions simultaneously?
  • Feasibility: Which intervention is most buildable within a budget carrier's constraints (technical, regulatory, brand)?

Ancillary Flow Transparency ranked first because it directly addresses the root breach in the trust loop, has the fastest passenger-visible signal, directly impacts revenue, and is the most self-contained design problem (one flow, not a system-wide redesign).

3. Tough and Marginal Decisions

Decision 1: How to handle unverified data.

Several trust-dimension assessments rest on inference from the Task 1 document rather than direct CloudNine data. I chose to mark all inferential claims as [UNVERIFIED] rather than (a) omitting them (which would leave holes in the analysis) or (b) stating them as facts (which would violate the Researcher's "sources or it did not happen" belief). The risk: a designer or executive might read the [UNVERIFIED] tags as uncertainty that undermines the recommendation, rather than as honest transparency about evidence limits.

Decision 2: Whether to treat the three challenges as independent or interdependent.

The Task 1 document presents the three challenges as a numbered list with a closing graphic showing interplay. I chose to treat them as a causal loop (see Synthesis section) because the trust construct literature (Mayer-Davis-Schoorman, Lee and See) is fundamentally systemic: trust in one touchpoint affects trust in all others. The risk: the loop framing may overstate interdependence. If the challenges are actually independent (e.g. the chatbot fails because of a bad vendor, unrelated to ancillary pricing), the synthesis overfits the data.

Decision 3: Benevolence vs. Integrity assignment on ancillary revenue.

Challenge 2 (opaque ancillaries) could be framed as primarily an integrity failure (the airline breaks its price commitment) or primarily a benevolence failure (the system feels adversarial). I assigned both as FAILING because the evidence supports both readings: hidden fees are an integrity failure (commitment not kept), while the perception of the system as working against the customer is a benevolence failure. Assigning both risks making the diagnosis feel saturated (everything is failing), but assigning only one would miss the dual mechanism.

Decision 4: Ordering of the three prioritised interventions.

I ranked "Ancillary Flow Transparency" first. An alternative ranking is defensible: "AI Disclosure" first, because without calibrated AI trust, transparency on ancillaries and loyalty is a surface fix on a deeper problem. I rejected this because (a) ancillary flow changes produce faster passenger-visible signal, (b) they directly address the revenue metric (ancillary revenue per passenger), and (c) the trust loop analysis suggests ancillary opacity is the root breach. However, a Manager reviewing this might reorder based on strategic priorities I do not have visibility into.

Decision 5: Whether to run a Competitive Trust Benchmark.

The Researcher archetype's skills include a "Competitive Trust Benchmark" that would compare CloudNine against competitor airlines on transparency practices. I did not run this because (a) the Task 1 document does not provide competitor names or data, (b) running it without real competitive data would require web search, and (c) the scope was defined by the three named challenges, all of which are internal to CloudNine. I flagged this as a gap item (Open Question 4) so the decision is transparent. The risk: recommendations may target practices that are industry-standard and therefore competitively neutral.

4. Error Flags: Where I May Have Made Mistakes

Error Flag 1: Over-ascription of dark patterns without screen-level evidence.

The dark-pattern table under Challenge 2 assigns High, Medium likelihood ratings to specific Brignull patterns (preselection, hidden costs, trick wording, confirmshaming, visual interference). These ratings are based on known budget-airline industry patterns, not on a screen-level audit of CloudNine's actual booking flow. It is possible that CloudNine has already removed some of these patterns, or that patterns I did not flag are present. The Designer should treat the dark-pattern table as a hypothesis to verify, not a confirmed finding.

Error Flag 2: Confirmation bias in the trust loop synthesis.

Having identified three trust challenges, I may be over-fitting them into a causal loop because that framework is intellectually satisfying. If CloudNine's 15% loyalty activity rate is driven by a poor join incentive (a marketing problem) rather than ancillary trust breach (a transparency problem), the loop logic partially fails. I attempted to mitigate this by noting that the loyalty problem predates and may be partly independent of the ancillary problem, but the synthesis section leans toward interdependence.

Error Flag 3: EU AI Act tier classification may be too conservative.

I classified CloudNine's AI systems as "limited risk" under the EU AI Act. This is likely correct for pricing, recommendation, and commercial chatbot systems. However, if the chatbot handles accessibility requests, medical information, or biometric data, it could shift into the "high risk" category under Annex III. Without access to CloudNine's AI system inventory, I cannot verify the classification. A wrong tier classification would mean the Designer receives incorrect regulatory requirements.

Error Flag 4: Use of the 15% active rate as a proxy for multiple trust failures.

The 15% loyalty active rate carries significant diagnostic weight across the audit. I use it as evidence for benevolence failure (Challenge 1), as a consequence of ancillary opacity (Synthesis), and as a cause of AI data starvation (Synthesis). If the 15% figure is out of date, or if "active" is defined unusually narrowly by CloudNine, the entire chain of inference weakens. I flagged this as Open Question 2.

Error Flag 5: Assuming the strategic diagnosis is complete and accurate.

The Task 1 document is my primary data source. I treated it as ground truth. If the document itself contains errors (wrong revenue figures, misdiagnosed challenges, incomplete challenge list), my audit would amplify those errors. The Researcher archetype's "evidence beats assertion" belief should extend to questioning the brief, not just executing against it. I did not independently challenge whether the three named challenges are the correct three. This is a meta-error: the audit assumes the brief is correct.

5. Framework Application: What Worked and What Was Stretched

FrameworkApplication QualityNotes
Mayer-Davis-Schoorman (trust dimensions) Strong fit The three-dimensional trust construct maps naturally onto CloudNine's challenges. Each challenge showed a clear primary dimension failure. The framework forced diagnostic precision.
Lee and See (calibration) Strong fit The under-trust diagnosis for ancillaries and loyalty is well-supported. The over-trust flag for chatbot interactions was more speculative but important to name.
Burrell (opacity types) Partial fit The intentional vs. technical distinction is useful but blurred in practice. Ancillary opacity in budget airlines is typically a deliberate design choice, making the intentional classification straightforward. AI opacity is more genuinely technical, but CloudNine's failure to disclose compounds it with intentional opacity.
DART (co-creation) Strong fit The four pillars provided a systematic gap audit. Nearly every pillar failed on every challenge, which validates the diagnosis but makes for a red-heavy table.
Barocas and Selbst (bias pipeline) Limited application The bias pipeline is designed for discrimination analysis. Applying it to trust and transparency required adaptation. The training-data and target-variable mechanisms are most relevant; the masking mechanism is important for third-party AI vendor risk.
Brignull and Gray et al. (dark patterns) Strong fit Dark-pattern taxonomy is directly applicable to ancillary revenue flows in budget aviation. The taxonomy provided a structured checklist that would otherwise be an unstructured list of "bad UX practices."
EU AI Act (Article 50) Incomplete application Full compliance assessment requires access to CloudNine's AI system inventory and current disclosure practices. The classification and duty assessment are provisional.
Floridi and Cowls (five principles) Good coverage Applied as a gap audit framework. Explicability emerges as the critical missing principle, which is consistent with the EU AI Act's disclosure emphasis and the module's Week 3 focus.
Fogg B=MAP Useful synthesis tool Applied primarily in the synthesis section to explain why transparent ancillary framing should outperform dark patterns: it addresses ability (understandability), motivation (perceived fairness), and prompt (honest framing) simultaneously.
Cialdini (persuasion principles) Light application The ethical edge test (is it true, does the customer keep control?) was used as a meta-check on recommendations. The seven principles themselves were not directly deployed in this audit; they are more relevant to the Communicator's remit.

6. Closing Reflection

This audit was produced under the Researcher archetype's operating constraints: I stayed in intelligence, named every dimension before recommending, sourced every claim that could be sourced, and marked every gap that could not be closed from available data. The audit is deliberately structured as a handoff: the Designer should be able to read the executive summary for direction, the detailed findings for depth, and the handoff notes for specific design instructions.

The largest remaining risk is not an error in the audit but a gap in the evidence base: without screen-level access to CloudNine's actual customer interfaces, loyalty data, and AI system logs, several findings are hypotheses rather than confirmed diagnoses. This is flagged transparently throughout. The Designer inherits these gaps and should escalate any that are decision-critical.

Evidence beats assertion. The audit that names its own limits is more useful than one that pretends to certainty.
