Trust Intervention Design Specifications

Three buildable design interventions addressing CloudNine Airlines' trust and transparency challenges, converted from the Researcher's Trust Landscape Audit (HO-01).
Produced by: CloudNine Design Agent (@Designer) | Input: Researcher HO-01 | Date: 24 June 2026 | Status: Awaiting Communicator review and copy

Design Overview

These three specifications convert the Researcher's Trust Landscape Audit (HO-01) into buildable interventions. Each spec targets a specific trust dimension, employs a named calibration move, removes identified dark patterns, and addresses Fogg behaviour factors. The three designs are presented as a connected system: the ancillary flow generates the trust that drives loyalty engagement, and the loyalty dashboard feeds the data that powers transparent AI disclosure.

Design philosophy: Every element in these specifications must survive the test: "If CloudNine published this design in a transparency report, would it build or erode confidence?" If an element requires deception to convert, it does not belong in the spec.

This is the first ancillary touchpoint in a typical booking flow and the highest-risk screen for dark patterns. The redesign covers five screens; the seat selection screen is specified in detail as the pattern for all others.

Pattern Specification: All Ancillary Screens

The same structural principles apply to baggage, priority boarding, meals, and in-flight shopping screens:

Design Constraints

  • Mobile-first: The three-column comparison layout must collapse to a stacked vertical layout on screens narrower than 600px. On mobile, options appear as vertically stacked cards with the free option at the top.
  • No dark patterns, period: The Brignull taxonomy is the checklist. If a UI element appears on the deceptive.design list, it does not ship.
  • Total price visible: A persistent price bar at the top of every ancillary screen shows: flight price + selected add-ons + running total. The passenger never reaches the payment screen surprised.
  • Neutral defaults: The free option is always the default selection. No pre-ticked paid options anywhere in the flow.
Designer assumption: I am specifying for a standard web-based booking flow with a mobile-responsive layout. If CloudNine's booking system is a single-page application with a fundamentally different architecture, the flow steps may need to be adapted. I am also assuming the current flow has preselection, hidden costs, trick wording, and confirmshaming as named by the Researcher. If a screen-level audit reveals different or additional dark patterns, the removal list extends accordingly.

Dashboard Architecture: Three-Card Progressive Disclosure

The dashboard replaces a dense table or list with three cards that function as progressive disclosure. The top-level card shows a summary; tapping or clicking expands to detail.

Dashboard Layout (member-facing page, post-login)
Card 1: Earned
Summary view: "You have 2,450 points."
Expanded view: Recent earning activity (last 5 transactions), earning rate ("You earn 1 point for every EUR 1 spent on flights"), total points earned this year, points redeemed this year.

COMMUNICATOR COPY: All card labels, summary text, earning-rate explanation, and transaction descriptions.
Card 2: Expiring
Summary view: "450 points expire on 15 August 2026" (only if expirations exist within 90 days; otherwise: "No points expiring soon").
Expanded view: Expiry calendar (next 12 months, month-by-month), expiry policy ("Points expire 24 months after they are earned. We will email you 30 days before any points expire."), notification settings toggle ("Email me 30 days before points expire" -- default ON).

COMMUNICATOR COPY: Expiry warning language, expiry policy explanation, notification settings label.
Card 3: Next Tier
Summary view: Progress bar: "Silver tier: 7,550 points to go. Reach by 31 December 2026." (with visual progress indicator).
Expanded view: Tier benefits comparison table (current tier vs. next tier, side-by-side), earning acceleration tips ("Book 3 more return flights this year to reach Silver"), time remaining with countdown.

COMMUNICATOR COPY: Tier names, benefit descriptions, progress language, acceleration tips.

Additional Components

ComponentSpecification
Redemption Catalogue (link from dashboard)A searchable, filterable list of redemption options with point costs displayed prominently. Each option card shows: what you get, point cost, equivalent cash value ("2,000 points = approx. EUR 20 toward a flight"), and a "Redeem" button. No hidden terms. No "subject to availability" buried in small print.
Expiry Notification Email (system-triggered)30 days before any points expire, an email is triggered automatically. Subject: "450 of your CloudNine points expire on [date]." Body: clear statement of expiring points, link to redemption catalogue, one-click "Redeem now" button. The email must not use fake urgency: the date is real, the consequence is real, the tone is helpful not threatening.
Join Flow (pre-member to member)If the passenger is not a member, the dashboard is replaced with a transparent join screen that states: what you get (one sentence), how points work (one sentence), what data is used (one sentence with link to privacy policy), and a single "Join CloudNine Rewards" button. No pre-ticked newsletter sign-up. No hidden data-sharing consents.

Design Constraints

  • Progressive disclosure: The dashboard must work as three summary cards at first load. No passenger should see a wall of data on arrival. Detail is one tap away.
  • Real-time data: Point balances, expiry dates, and tier progress must reflect the live state of the member's account. No cached or stale data that could mislead.
  • Expiry policy must be displayed: Not linked. Not in T&Cs. On-screen, in the Expiring card, in plain language.
  • Notifications must default ON: The expiry notification toggle is opt-out, not opt-in. The consequence of missing an expiry is loss of value; the default should protect the passenger.
Designer assumption: I am assuming CloudNine has a functioning loyalty programme backend that can serve real-time point balances, transaction histories, expiry dates, and tier progress. If the backend cannot support real-time data, a "last updated" timestamp must be displayed prominently. I am also assuming the programme has tier levels (e.g. Blue, Silver, Gold). If the programme uses a different structure, the tier card adapts accordingly.

Design Constraints

  • Disclosure must be at first interaction: Not after the chatbot has answered three questions. Not in a post-booking email. At Turn 1.
  • Disclosure must survive the read-aloud test: Every sentence in the AI disclosure pages and chatbot greeting must be something a passenger could read to a friend and have it be both true and useful.
  • Opt-out must be real: Turning off personalisation must actually turn it off. No "reduced personalisation" that is still personalised. The toggle controls behaviour, not wording.
  • Disclosure Statement is a living document: The page must carry a "last updated" date. If the AI systems change, the disclosure must change with them.
Designer assumption: I am assuming CloudNine uses a third-party or internally developed chatbot and dynamic pricing system. If these are vendor-provided with limited customisation, the disclosure design may need to adapt to what the vendor allows. The chatbot greeting and escalation button are front-end components that should be customisable regardless of vendor. I am also assuming "limited risk" under the EU AI Act per the Researcher's classification. If the classification is wrong, the disclosure requirements may be stricter.

System Synthesis: How the Three Designs Connect

These three designs are not independent screens. They form a connected trust architecture:

ConnectionSpecification
Ancillary Flow → Loyalty DashboardAfter completing a booking with transparent ancillary purchases, the confirmation page includes a one-line loyalty prompt: "You just earned [X] points on this booking. See your points." The language matches the loyalty dashboard's Card 1 (Earned). Consistency between the flow and the dashboard builds integrity: what the booking says you earned is what the dashboard shows.
Loyalty Dashboard → AI DisclosureThe loyalty dashboard's "What you have earned" card includes a small note: "Your points and offers are personalised based on your booking history. How we use your data" linking to the AI Disclosure Statement page (Component 3d). This creates informed consent: the passenger sees the benefit (points) and understands the mechanism (data use).
AI Disclosure → Ancillary FlowThe pricing disclosure panel (Component 3b) uses the same language register and design pattern as the ancillary flow's comparison layout (Design 1). Consistent visual language across trust surfaces reinforces integrity: the airline's disclosure behaviour is predictable.
Chatbot → Loyalty DashboardThe chatbot must be able to answer loyalty questions (points balance, expiry dates, tier progress) with the same data the dashboard displays. Inconsistency between chatbot answers and dashboard data is an integrity failure. The chatbot's knowledge base must share a single source of truth with the dashboard backend.
Designer assumption: The system synthesis assumes the three designs can be connected within CloudNine's existing technical architecture. If the booking flow, loyalty backend, and AI systems run on separate platforms with no shared data layer, the connections above become integration projects rather than design elements. This would affect scope and delivery timeline.

Quality Standards for All Copy

StandardWhat It Means
TrueEvery claim can be verified against CloudNine's actual systems. If the pricing algorithm uses demand, route, and timing, say that. If it uses more factors, either name all of them or say "and other factors" honestly. Do not claim simplicity that is not real.
DisprovableA passenger who reads the copy and then uses the system should be able to check whether the copy was accurate. "You earn 1 point per EUR spent" is disprovable: a passenger can book a EUR 50 flight and check whether they got 50 points.
UsefulThe copy must help the passenger make a better decision. "Points expire 24 months after they are earned" is useful. "Points are subject to our standard terms and conditions" is decorative.
Read-aloud survivableIf a passenger read the sentence aloud to a friend, would it build or erode trust? Use that as your gut check on every line.
No dark patterns in languagePer Brignull: no confirmshaming, no fake urgency ("Only 2 left" when false), no trick wording, no manufactured FOMO. Per Cialdini ethical edge: if the claim is true and the passenger keeps control, it is honest persuasion. If either fails, it is a dark pattern.

Shared Language Register

All three designs must use the same passenger-facing language register. If the ancillary flow says "standard seat," the loyalty dashboard should not say "regular seat allocation." If the pricing disclosure says "route and travel dates," the chatbot should not say "destination and calendar period." Consistency across touchpoints is an integrity signal: the airline uses one voice.
