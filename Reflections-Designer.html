2. Thought Process: How the Design Specs Were Built

Step 1: Select the Design Approach

The Researcher delivered three opportunities with a priority ranking. I chose to design all three as a connected system because the Researcher's trust-deficit-loop analysis (HO-01 Synthesis section) makes a compelling case that designing them independently would leave structural gaps. The alternative (design only Priority 1 and defer 2 and 3) was rejected because the designs are interdependent: the ancillary flow's transparent pricing language must be consistent with the loyalty dashboard's earning-rate language and the AI disclosure's pricing explanation.

Step 2: Establish the Design Pattern

Each design spec follows a consistent internal contract:

  1. Name the trust dimension targeted (ability, benevolence, integrity per Mayer-Davis-Schoorman).
  2. Name the calibration move (show uncertainty, offer comparison, progressive disclosure per Lee and See).
  3. Name the dark patterns removed (specific Brignull types).
  4. Name the Fogg factors addressed (motivation, ability, prompt).
  5. Run the Cialdini ethical edge check (true? customer keeps control?).
  6. Specify the screens and interactions (wireframe descriptions, flow steps).
  7. Name the constraints.
  8. Flag assumptions.

This pattern ensures every design is traceable to the Researcher's diagnostic framework and defensible against the module's ethical standards.

Step 3: Design 1 (Ancillary Flow) as the Reference Implementation

I designed the ancillary flow first and in the most detail because (a) it is the highest priority, (b) it establishes the visual and language patterns the other two designs reference, and (c) it is the most self-contained design (one flow, six to eight screens). The seat selection screen was chosen as the detailed wireframe because it is the first ancillary touchpoint in a typical booking flow and the highest-risk screen for dark patterns (preselection, trick wording, confirmshaming). The pattern established on this screen (three-column comparison, neutral defaults, persistent price bar, dignified decline options) propagates to all other ancillary screens.

Step 4: Design 2 (Loyalty Dashboard) as Progressive Disclosure Architecture

The loyalty dashboard was the most challenging design because it must serve two audiences: the 15% active members (who need detail) and the 85% inactive members (whose first encounter with the dashboard might be the join screen). The three-card progressive-disclosure architecture solves this: summary view is accessible to both groups, expanded view rewards engagement. The join-flow component was added to close the gap the Researcher identified: a passenger who encounters the dashboard and is not a member should see an immediate, transparent path to join, not an error state or a wall of empty data.

Step 5: Design 3 (AI Disclosure) as a Distributed System

Unlike the first two designs, which are contained screens (one flow, one dashboard), AI disclosure must be distributed across four touchpoints: chatbot (turn-1 greeting), pricing (collapsible panel), recommendations (opt-out toggle), and a standalone page (full disclosure statement). The design challenge was consistency: the same language register, the same visual patterns, the same trust signals across components that live in different parts of the product. The shared constraint (EU AI Act Article 50: disclosure at first interaction, clear and accessible) provides the unifying thread.

Step 6: System Synthesis and Communicator Handoff

The final step was to identify the connection points between designs (e.g. ancillary flow feeding loyalty dashboard, loyalty dashboard linking to AI disclosure) and to structure the Communicator handoff so that the copy slots are clearly identified and the quality standards (true, disprovable, useful, read-aloud survivable, no dark patterns in language) are explicit. The COMMUNICATOR COPY slots were embedded inline with the designs rather than listed separately so the Communicator sees the copy in its design context, not as an abstract list.

3. Tough and Marginal Decisions

Decision 1: Whether to design all three interventions or stop at Priority 1.

The Researcher's priority sequence is clear: Ancillary Flow first, Loyalty second, AI Disclosure third. I could have delivered only Design 1 and deferred 2 and 3. I chose to deliver all three because the Researcher's synthesis establishes that the challenges form a self-reinforcing loop. Delivering only one intervention leaves the loop intact. The risk: delivering three designs without user testing data means all three carry the same assumptions and evidence gaps. Three uncertain designs may be worse than one focused, testable design.

Decision 2: The "free option as default" rule.

I specified that the free option is always the default selection across all ancillary screens. This is a strong constraint that may reduce short-term ancillary conversion. The alternative (no default, forced active choice) was considered but rejected because forced choice on every screen increases friction and may feel coercive in its own right. The free-default rule aligns with the Designer's core belief that "a design that requires deception to convert has not earned its conversion." The risk: if CloudNine's ancillary revenue depends significantly on preselection-driven conversion, this design may not be commercially viable without a complementary pricing or bundling strategy that I have not specified.

Decision 3: Expiry notification default ON vs. opt-in.

The loyalty dashboard specification sets expiry notifications to default ON. This is a benevolence move: the consequence of missing an expiry is value loss for the passenger. An opt-in approach (default OFF) would be more libertarian but would also mean most passengers never enable the notification and lose points silently. I chose default ON because the benevolent position, per the Mayer-Davis-Schoorman framework, is to protect the passenger from a known harm. The risk: if CloudNine's email volume is already high, adding default-ON notifications could increase unsubscribe rates, which has its own downstream effects on communication reach.

Decision 4: Mobile-first with three-column collapse.

The ancillary flow comparison layout is a three-column design on desktop that collapses to stacked vertical cards on mobile. This is a structural decision with significant implementation implications. The alternative (always vertical, single-column on all devices) would be simpler to build but loses the comparison benefit on desktop, where side-by-side comparison is the most effective calibration move (Lee and See: offer comparison). I chose the responsive approach because budget airline passengers book on both mobile and desktop, and the design should serve both contexts well. The risk: the responsive behaviour adds build complexity and testing surface area.

Decision 5: Chatbot greeting structure (four elements in Turn 1).

The chatbot specification requires self-identification, capability statement, limit statement, and escalation path all in Turn 1, before any user input. This is a heavy first message and may reduce engagement compared to a friendlier, lighter greeting. I chose full disclosure because EU AI Act Article 50 requires identification at first interaction, and the Researcher identified chatbot over-trust as a calibration risk (passengers accepting answers without verification). A lighter greeting would be more engaging but less calibrated. The risk: the greeting length may cause passengers to skip past it without reading, defeating its purpose. The Communicator's copy skill is critical here: the greeting must be comprehensive but scannable.

4. Error Flags: Where I May Have Made Mistakes

Error Flag 1: Designing against inferred dark patterns, not confirmed ones.

My dark-pattern removal list for Design 1 is based on the Researcher's inferential dark-pattern table, which is itself based on industry patterns in budget aviation, not a screen-level audit of CloudNine's actual booking flow. If CloudNine's flow does not contain preselection, hidden costs, trick wording, or confirmshaming, I have designed solutions for problems that do not exist. Conversely, if CloudNine's flow contains dark patterns the Researcher did not identify (e.g. "bait and switch" pricing where the advertised fare is unavailable), my design does not address them. The Communicator should run their Communication Ethics Audit skill against the actual flow before writing copy.

Error Flag 2: The "connected system" assumption may over-integrate.

I designed the three interventions as a connected system with cross-references (ancillary flow feeds loyalty data, loyalty dashboard links to AI disclosure, etc.). If CloudNine's booking system, loyalty backend, and AI platform are separate systems with different vendors, update cycles, and data formats, the connections I specified may not be technically feasible within a reasonable build timeline. The designs work independently (each is self-contained); the connections are additive. But I presented them as integral, which could mislead about build complexity.

Error Flag 3: No accessibility specification.

None of the three designs include accessibility requirements (screen reader compatibility, keyboard navigation, colour contrast, text sizing). This is an omission. In a production design spec for a customer-facing airline, WCAG 2.1 AA compliance would be a baseline requirement. I did not include it because (a) the Researcher's audit did not flag accessibility as a trust dimension, and (b) the spec is focused on trust and transparency design patterns. However, inaccessible design is a trust failure: a passenger who cannot use the interface cannot calibrate trust. The Maker or a subsequent design pass should add accessibility requirements.

Error Flag 4: The "no dark patterns" constraint may be too absolute.

I specified "If a UI element appears on the deceptive.design list, it does not ship." This is a strong, simple rule. In practice, some patterns exist on a spectrum. A pre-selected "remember me" checkbox on a login screen is technically preselection but is a usability convention, not a dark pattern. A countdown timer that reflects a real promotion deadline is scarcity but is honest if the deadline is real. My spec does not distinguish between dark patterns and conventions that look similar but are ethically neutral. The Communicator or a design reviewer should apply the Cialdini ethical edge test (is it true + does the customer keep control?) to individual elements rather than blanket-banning pattern names.

Error Flag 5: The Communicator handoff assumes the Communicator can write copy without user-testing data.

I have asked the Communicator to fill every COMMUNICATOR COPY slot with language that passes the true/disprovable/useful test. But the Communicator, like me, does not have access to CloudNine's actual systems, customer vocabulary, or user-testing data. The copy will be written based on the same inferential evidence base that informed my designs. If the Communicator cannot verify a claim (e.g. the actual loyalty earning rate, the actual pricing algorithm factors, the actual chatbot capability), the copy will carry the same assumption risk as my designs. This is not an error I can fix; it is a systemic limitation of the pipeline's evidence access. The Communicator should flag any copy elements that depend on unverified assumptions.

Error Flag 6: No specification for A/B testing or measurement.

The designs are specified as complete interventions, but I did not include measurement plans. The Researcher's role includes signal evaluation (kill/pivot/scale after seven days of data), but I did not specify what data the designs should emit for evaluation. For example: the ancillary flow should log conversion rate per option, time spent on screen, and abandonment rate at the ancillary step. The loyalty dashboard should log card-expand rate, redemption click-through, and notification engagement. Without measurement instrumentation, the Researcher cannot evaluate whether the designs work. This is an oversight in my handoff.

5. Closing Reflection

These three design specifications were produced under the Designer archetype's operating constraints: I translated intelligence into structures, named every trust dimension and calibration move, removed dark patterns systematically, and handed off with an unambiguous contract for the Communicator. I did not write copy (that is the Communicator's lane), I did not build code (that is the Maker's lane), and I did not decide strategy (that is yours).

The largest remaining risk is the evidence gap inherited from the Researcher: without screen-level access to CloudNine's actual interfaces, these designs are hypotheticals built on inferred problems. They are academically grounded, ethically defensible, and structurally sound. But they have not been tested against the reality of CloudNine's booking flow, loyalty backend, or AI systems. The pipeline's "build, then distrust" instruction (Week 4 V4) applies: these designs should be reviewed against live CloudNine interfaces, and any divergence between the assumed problem and the actual problem should trigger a redesign cycle.

Constraints are gifts; name them out loud. The constraint here is incomplete evidence. I have named it. The Communicator inherits it. The decision to proceed, pause, or seek additional research is yours.
